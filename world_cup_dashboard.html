About This Model

Betting & Predictions

This model powers predictions for every World Cup game and helps identify the most probable outcomes across all 104 matches and all 500,000+ simulations. Use this to inform your betting strategy, but please gamble responsibly!

The ELO System

I created a custom ELO rating model based on all international football matches since 1981. Teams start at 1500 points, and ratings evolve naturally through every match played chronologically. Each result shifts ratings based on expected outcomes—upsets grant bigger points, expected wins grant fewer.

The ELO ratings shown here use the average of the last 3 months (5 games) before the tournament. This balances recent form with consistency, rather than using the absolute latest snapshot.

From Ratings to Expected Goals

The model doesn't directly predict wins—it predicts goals. Each team starts with a baseline of 1.35 expected goals per match. The ELO difference between teams is converted into an exponential scale: a stronger team's expected output increases while the weaker team's decreases in mirror.

Example: Spain (1932.75) vs Curaçao (1500.91)
The model estimates Spain to score ~4.7 expected goals, while Curaçao ~0.4. But that's just the mean—randomness comes next.

Adding Realism: Poisson Distribution

Expected goals don't determine the outcome. Instead, each team's actual score is drawn from a Poisson distribution, the standard way to model discrete events (like goals) in a fixed time window. Spain's 4.68 expected goals might become 2, 4, 6, or even 0. Curaçao's 0.39 usually means 0, but occasionally surprises with a goal.

This is what allows upsets. Even the most dominant teams can have quiet matches, and underdogs can find the breakthrough.

Tournament Simulation at Scale

To predict the full tournament, I run every single match through this ELO → expected goals → Poisson pipeline. Group tables build from scratch. Qualifiers are ranked on points and goal difference. The knockout stage advances the 8 best third-placed teams using FIFA's official rules (a 495-row lookup table mapping every possible combination).

One simulation gives one outcome. Sometimes Scotland wins. Usually it doesn't.

To get meaningful probabilities, I ran 500,000 complete simulations. Every team's chance to reach each stage, win the tournament, or claim third place is derived from how often that outcome occurred across all half-million runs.

Curaçao: The Twin Miracles of a 1-in-250,000 2026 World Cup Win

Out of 500,000 simulated 2026 World Cups, Curaçao lifted the trophy exactly twice. Not once in a generation. Not once in a century. Twice in half a million tournaments - two separate universes where football itself seemed to break. Both these journeys to conquer the world are absolutely incredible.

Curaçao is a Caribbean island located around 37 kilometres off the coast of Venezuela. It has a population of just 156,000 people - about the size of Oxford, a city whose university alone has more than 35,000 students. In 2026, they became the smallest nation ever to qualify for a FIFA World Cup. Despite their size, they aren't without talent. Their squad includes players such as Tahith Chong, formerly of Manchester United and now at Sheffield United, alongside winger Sontje Hansen. But compared to the traditional giants of international football, they remain massive outsiders, and these players will have to be playing out of their skin across the tournament. In my ELO mode (see more on About page) they were the worst ranked team in the tournament.

Curaçao’s group in the 2026 World Cup consists of 4 time world cup winning giants Germany, Ecuador who are undefeated internationally in the past 19 games entering the tournament, and Ivory Coast, Afcon Champions in 2023.

Let us explore the two simulations where they defied all odds to reach the final!

Simulation 170,000/500,000

Simulation 170,000 / 500,000 produces one of the most structurally extreme tournament paths in the dataset, defined less by sustained dominance and more by repeated survival against elite opposition, almost entirely through penalty shootouts.

The group stage begins with an immediate disruption of the expected hierarchy. Curaçao began insanely, with a 2–1 win over Germany, a result that appears in both of the World Cup winning runs. In parallel, Ecuador defeated the Ivory Coast, reinforcing a tightly contested group structure. Curaçao then suffered a 4–0 defeat to Ecuador and drew 1–1 with the Ivory Coast, finishing with just four points but still advancing in third place.

Curaçao defeated South Africa 2–0, while across the wider bracket, multiple traditional favourites advance as expected, briefly stabilising the tournament structure before the knockout rounds intensify. This is where it gets truly crazy.

The Round of 16 marks the first large-scale collapse of pre-tournament hierarchy. Curaçao progresses after eliminating England via a 2–2 draw and penalties, what an unbelievable result by the way, while simultaneously Germany knocks out France, and Colombia eliminate Spain.

The quarter-finals venture further into crazy. Curaçao draw 2–2 with Brazil and win the shootout, eliminating one of the most statistically dominant sides. At this point, Curaçao have removed both England and Brazil without winning a knockout match in regulation time.

In the semi-finals, they hold reigning champions Argentina to a 0–0 draw and once again eliminate them on penalties, meaning the defending champions exit the competition. Meanwhile, Germany progresses on the other side of the bracket by beating Senegal, setting up a rematch of the group stage upset.

The final becomes even more bonkers, where another 0-0 draw results in Germany and Curaçao going to penalties. And Curaçao win the tournament on penalties. This completes a sequence in which Curaçao won four consecutive knockout shootouts against England, Brazil, Argentina, and Germany in both the group stage context and the final. They finished the tournament as champions, with a minus 1 goal difference!

Simulation 380,000/500,000

Group Table — Simulation 380,000

In the opening game, Curaçao faced Germany, who were expected to win the group comfortably. At the same time, Ecuador beat Ivory Coast, meaning the group was suddenly alive in ways nobody predicted. What followed was the first miracle of the simulation. Curaçao stunned Germany 2–1.

However their fortune did not go on. Curaçao then lost in heavily, 3–1 to Ecuador, followed by a 1–0 loss to the Ivory Coast. Curaçao progress in third place. At the same time, across the tournament, Argentina - another heavyweight - were already in trouble, drawing their opening two matches before collapsing in their final game to exit the competition early. One of the tournament favourites had already been dragged into chaos - and Curaçao were still alive.

In the Round of 32. England were eliminated 2–0 by Uzbekistan in one of the biggest shocks of the round. Portugal were dismantled 3–0 by Panama. Brazil, one of the pre-tournament favourites, were knocked out on penalties by Japan. Germany eliminated France. The tournament was turning into a graveyard for giants. Curaçao’s test in this round was Mexico, who had been flawless in their group, finishing with nine points and looking every bit a title contender after sweeping past South Africa, South Korea and the Czech Republic. They were expected to end Curaçao’s run immediately. After 90 minutes, it was 1–1. Nothing separated them. Penalties followed. Curaçao won.

By now, the tournament landscape was unrecognisable. With England, Brazil, Portugal, France and Argentina already gone, the path to the final was suddenly open in a way no simulation had predicted. In the Round of 16, Curaçao faced Uzbekistan - one of the surprise teams of the tournament. A disciplined defensive performance and a single decisive goal secured a 1–0 win, sending Curaçao into the quarter-finals. At the same stage, Colombia eliminated Spain 1–0, while the Netherlands and Germany remained among the last traditional powers standing.

Then came the rematch. Quarter-finals. Curaçao vs Germany. The same Germany that they had stunned in the group stage. Germany arrived for revenge. The match was tense and controlled, but once again, neither side could be separated. It ended 1–1. Once again, penalties decided everything. Once again, Curaçao prevailed. Ecuador simultaneously knocked out the Netherlands 1–0, removing another European heavyweight.

The semi-final brought Canada, another unexpected survivor, beating Uruguay, Paraguay and Egypt to reach that stage. In a tight, nervy match, they edged a 1–0 victory to reach the World Cup final - a stage they were never meant to see. Waiting for them in the final was Iran. Iran had topped a difficult group containing Belgium, Egypt and New Zealand without defeat, then continued through Cabo Verde, Iraq, Colombia and Ecuador. They entered the final as unlikely favourites. In the final, Curaçao produced their most complete performance of the entire simulation. For the first time since the group stage, they scored twice, shutting the game down completely.

Curaçao 2–0 Iran. World champions. All with a goal difference of 0 entering the final!

Simulation Summary

Across both simulations, Curaçao’s runs highlight just how chaotic and beautifully unrealistic football can become when scaled across hundreds of thousands of tournament outcomes. In both universes, they defy the structural logic of elite international football — repeatedly eliminating giants like Germany, Brazil, Argentina, England, and Mexico despite consistently being underdogs in every underlying metric. The pathways are statistically extreme: heavy reliance on knockout penalties, narrow margins in decisive matches, and timing that consistently favours survival over dominance. Yet that is exactly what makes the model compelling — it doesn’t just produce likely winners, it occasionally generates absurd, cinematic timelines where the weakest team in the tournament bends probability just enough to become world champions.
